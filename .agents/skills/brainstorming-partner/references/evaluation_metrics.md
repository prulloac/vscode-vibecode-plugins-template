# Evaluation Metrics

This reference defines the three core metrics used to assess generated ideas: Feasibility, Impact, and Novelty.

## Feasibility (0-10 scale)

**Definition**: How practical and actionable is the idea given current constraints, resources, and capabilities?

### Scoring Rubric

- **9-10**: Can be implemented immediately with existing resources and knowledge
- **7-8**: Achievable with some additional resources, effort, or learning
- **5-6**: Possible but requires significant effort, new skills, or external dependencies
- **3-4**: Theoretically possible but very difficult, expensive, or requires major changes
- **1-2**: Extremely difficult or unrealistic given practical constraints
- **0**: Impossible or violates fundamental constraints

### Considerations

- Technical complexity
- Resource requirements (time, budget, people)
- Existing infrastructure/knowledge
- Regulatory or legal constraints
- Dependency on external factors
- Internal expertise and capabilities

### Assessment Approach

Consider the current context: What resources are realistically available? What's the timeline? What's the organization's risk tolerance?

## Impact (0-10 scale)

**Definition**: How significant and valuable would the idea be if successfully implemented?

### Scoring Rubric

- **9-10**: Transformational—solves major problems or creates substantial new value
- **7-8**: Significant—meaningfully improves situation or creates notable benefits
- **5-6**: Moderate—provides measurable benefits but not transformative
- **3-4**: Marginal—provides minor improvements or benefits
- **1-2**: Minimal—barely noticeable positive effects
- **0**: No positive impact or negative impact

### Considerations

- Value to users/customers/stakeholders
- Scope of benefit (how many affected?)
- Duration of benefit (temporary vs. lasting)
- Strategic alignment
- Revenue/cost implications
- Brand/reputation effects
- Competitive advantage

### Assessment Approach

Evaluate against specific success metrics: What would success look like? Who benefits? How much better is the situation?

## Novelty (0-10 scale)

**Definition**: How original and new is the idea within its context?

### Scoring Rubric

- **9-10**: Highly unique—truly original approach not previously tried in this space
- **7-8**: Original—novel combination or significant variation on existing approaches
- **5-6**: Somewhat new—adaptation of familiar approaches with new elements
- **3-4**: Incremental—minor variations on standard/existing approaches
- **1-2**: Minimal newness—obvious or widely-used approach
- **0**: Not new at all—direct copy of existing solution

### Considerations

- How different is this from current solutions?
- Have competitors or peers tried similar approaches?
- Does it combine existing ideas in a new way?
- Is it a new application of a known technique?
- Does it introduce genuinely new thinking?
- How will this differentiate from alternatives?

### Assessment Approach

Compare against: What exists today? What have others tried? What's the state of the industry?

## Composite Score

**Overall Score = (Feasibility + Impact + Novelty) / 3**

This gives a balanced evaluation considering:

- Can we do it? (Feasibility)
- Should we do it? (Impact)
- Is it worth doing? (Novelty)

### Interpretation

- **8-10**: Strong idea—pursue strongly
- **6-7.9**: Good idea—consider pursuing with conditions
- **5-5.9**: Fair idea—useful for specific contexts
- **3-4.9**: Weak idea—may need refinement
- **0-2.9**: Poor idea—likely not worth pursuing without major changes

## Evaluation Guidelines

### When Evaluating Ideas

1. **Be realistic but not pessimistic**: Consider actual constraints without being unnecessarily limiting
1. **Score independently**: Evaluate each metric separately before calculating composite
1. **Provide context**: For each metric, note key factors that influenced the score
1. **Use the rubric**: Refer to the specific scoring rubrics above
1. **Consider timing**: Impact and feasibility can depend on timeline

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Over-optimism**: Rating feasibility too high without considering real constraints
- **Under-innovation**: Penalizing novelty for ideas that are appropriately targeted
- **Context blindness**: Not considering the specific organization/market context
- **Anchoring**: Being influenced by previously scored ideas

### Scoring Tips

- **Feasibility**: Think about "how long would it take to implement?" and "what would it cost?"
- **Impact**: Ask "how would this change the situation?" and "who would benefit?"
- **Novelty**: Compare to "what already exists" and "what have we/they tried before?"

## Using Scores for Decision Making

### Filtering Ideas

- **Score 7+**: Recommended for immediate consideration
- **Score 5-7**: Conditional recommendations based on strategic priorities
- **Score \<5**: Archive for later or refine before pursuing

### Ranking Ideas

When comparing multiple ideas, the composite score provides a first-pass ranking, but consider:

- Ideas with high feasibility but lower impact (safe bets)
- Ideas with lower feasibility but transformational impact (moonshots)
- Portfolio balance (mix of incremental + transformational)

### Refinement Opportunities

Ideas with unbalanced scores suggest refinement:

- **Low feasibility, high impact**: Can you reduce complexity or increase resources?
- **High feasibility, low impact**: Can you increase scope or combine with other ideas?
- **Low novelty, high feasibility**: Is there a way to increase differentiation?
